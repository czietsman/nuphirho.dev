Novel Findings

This page records confirmed novel contributions from the nuphirho.dev research programme. A finding appears here only after a structured search across the primary literature for the relevant field, conducted independently by a minimum of three language models. Any counter-citations produced by external models are verified for existence before the novelty claim is assessed. Fabricated citations are logged separately and do not reduce a novelty claim.

Confidence levels: High (4/4 models, unanimous), Confirmed (3/3 models, unanimous), Provisional (3/3 models, but with a noted gap in search coverage warranting follow-up before strong publication claims).

The programme is ongoing. New findings are added as they are verified.

Cluster 3: Measurement theory and LLM evaluation

Finding 11: LLM evaluation as measurement of a latent quality vector via prompt-defined projections
The individual mathematical components exist in adjacent fields: measurement theory, psychometrics, signal processing, and compressed sensing. The synthesis connecting these to LLM evaluation as a formal measurement problem has not been published.
Search: four independent models, unanimous confirmation. Citation verification confirmed RepEval (arXiv:2404.19563) is not prior art: it uses projection in the sense of LLM hidden-state direction vectors, not governance-defined measurement subspaces. Finding 11 novelty is strengthened, not weakened, by this verification.
Confidence: High (4/4). Intended use: Paper.
Forthcoming: standalone measurement theory paper.

Finding 14: Scoring distribution as instrument reliability measure via framing-variation
Existing literature varies the model or the input when assessing evaluation reliability. No published work treats framing-variation of the evaluation instrument itself as the experimental variable for reliability measurement in LLM governance prompt evaluation. Grounded in Cronbach's Generalizability Theory.
Search: three independent models. Citation verification complete.
Confidence: Confirmed (3/3). Intended use: Paper.
Forthcoming: measurement theory paper, connected to Finding 11.

Finding 15: Fixed point convergence applied to evaluation instruments as operators on their own outputs
The mathematical concept of fixed-point convergence is well established. The specific application to evaluation instruments as operators whose recursive application must converge for instrument validity, linked to projection stability of a latent quality vector, has no verifiable prior art.

Cluster 4: Pyrrhonian epistemology and governance limits

Finding 10: Pyrrhonian criterion problem extended to formal AI governance systems
The Pyrrhonian regress of criteria, as developed by Sextus Empiricus, applies directly to the problem of governing AI governance harnesses: any criterion used to evaluate a specification requires a further criterion. The correct response is externalisation rather than suspension of judgement. Co-authored working paper in progress with Jaco Louw (Stellenbosch University).

Prior art note: Worley (2019, arXiv:1807.09836) connects Pyrrhonian epistemic circularity to AGI alignment and proposes containment via hinge propositions. This finding extends the argument: the representational limit is structural, applies to all formal governance systems, not only AGI, and is not containable by approximation. This is an extension, not a duplication.

Confidence: Confirmed (3/3). Intended use: Paper and book.

Cluster 6: Measurement methodology

Finding 17: Positive/negative complementary scoring as instrument calibration diagnostic
Score each quality principle twice: once as a positive statement and once as a negative statement of the same principle applied to the same document. The two scores should sum to 1.0. Deviation from this conservation constraint is a direct measure of instrument unreliability (framing bias in the rubric) rather than document quality. The three elements that constitute the novelty: complementary polarity scoring, a hard theoretical conservation constraint, and interpreting deviation as instrument noise. All three exist separately in prior literature. Their integration into a single operational diagnostic does not.
Note: "rubric consistency" is the preferred term to avoid confusion with ML calibration.
Search: four independent models, unanimous confirmation.
Confidence: High (4/4). Intended use: Paper.
Forthcoming: measurement theory paper, in conjunction with Findings 11 and 14.

Cluster 8: Agentic coordination

Finding 16: Silent specification drift as a coordination model governance failure mode
A coordination model degrades silently as the underlying AI models it coordinates are updated, fine-tuned, or deprecated, without the coordination layer detecting the change. BDD specifications continue to pass green, evidence manifests look correct, but the system produces outputs that no longer reflect original intent because the model changed, not the specification. This is distinct from concept drift, capability regression, and specification staleness. No prior art treats this as a coordination-model governance failure mode under any label.
Confidence: Confirmed (3/3). Intended use: Paper.
Forthcoming: PromptQ ACE research programme paper.

Cluster 9: Engineering discipline convergence

Finding 18: AI reduces the marginal cost of coordinating knowledge across engineering disciplines
Proper software engineering has always required mastery across more disciplines than any individual could reasonably hold: formal methods, testing theory, architecture, security, domain knowledge, governance. The cost of that coordination produced specialisation, and specialisation produced silos. AI reduces the marginal cost of holding these disciplines in coordination simultaneously. This is a different claim from "AI makes you an expert in everything." It addresses coordination cost, not knowledge acquisition cost. The synthesis connecting AI capability to a reduction in the knowledge coherence barrier, and its implications for a unified professional standard in AI-assisted engineering, has not been published in this form.
The strongest contrarian challenge: reducing coordination cost does not automatically produce professional standards without institutional enforcement. The liability barrier remains. This is the open question.
Search: three independent models, cross-disciplinary software engineering literature, professionalisation literature, AI capability research.
Confidence: Provisional (3/3). Intended use: Book and future paper.

Cluster 10: Cross-domain specification quality theory

Finding 19: PromptQ principles are a domain-agnostic completeness calculus for governance specifications
Four independent models confirmed that P1 through P6 appear across healthcare (AGREE II, GRADE, WHO surgical checklist), legal contracts (contra proferentem, Four Corners Rule), engineering specifications (IEEE 830, NASA FRET), and aviation and nuclear SOPs under different names. The convergence is unmistakable.
The most important theoretical implication: AI governance must have the most structurally complete governance model of any domain. Medicine, engineering, nuclear, and law all have institutional compensating mechanisms that fill specification gaps (courts, licensing boards, regulatory bodies, liability). AI agents have none of these. The governance document carries the full structural load alone.
Confidence: High (4/4 models). Intended use: Book (Part I), governance limits paper, public discourse.
